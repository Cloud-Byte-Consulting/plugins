## Executive Summary

Organizations today face two parallel, converging maturity curves: (1) **Internal Developer Platform (IDP) maturity** — the degree to which platform engineering, self-service infrastructure, and golden paths have been institutionalized — and (2) **Agentic Developer Platform (ADP) maturity** — the degree to which AI coding agents, agentic workflows, and agent governance have been adopted safely and at scale. Well-established frameworks exist for the first (CNCF's Platform Engineering Maturity Model, Team Topologies, DORA, SPACE, and vendor scorecard tools such as Cortex, OpsLevel, and Port.io), but no single, universally recognized maturity model yet exists for the second. Instead, a fast-evolving patchwork of vendor frameworks (DORA's 2025 AI Capabilities Model, Microsoft's Platform Engineering Capability Model with AI extensions, Cloud Security Alliance's MCP Security Maturity Model, Cortex's AI Readiness Bronze/Silver/Gold scorecard) is converging on similar dimensions: governance/security posture, data/context accessibility, tooling standardization, and measurement discipline ([CNCF](https://tag-app-delivery.cncf.io/wgs/platforms/maturity-model/readme/), [DORA 2025 Report](https://dora.dev/dora-report-2025/), [Cortex AI Readiness](https://docs.cortex.io/solutions/ai-readiness), [CSA MCP Security Maturity Model](https://labs.cloudsecurityalliance.org/agentic/agentic-mcp-security-best-practices-v1/)).

Automated AI-driven discovery of these maturity levels is technically feasible today by combining static repository analysis, cloud/API telemetry, and LLM-based summarization — and a nascent ecosystem of tools (engineering intelligence platforms, MCP security scanners, "repo readiness" assessment skills) already performs pieces of this work, though no vendor yet offers a single unified IDP+ADP maturity scanner. This report synthesizes existing frameworks, catalogs concrete measurable signals, proposes a unified maturity rubric, and recommends a practical multi-agent architecture for automated discovery and continuous scoring.

## 1. Existing Maturity Models and Frameworks for Platform Engineering and IDPs

### 1.1 CNCF Platform Engineering Maturity Model

The most authoritative and widely cited framework is the **CNCF TAG App Delivery Platform Engineering Maturity Model**, first published in October 2023 and iterated since by the CNCF Platforms Working Group ([CNCF announcement](https://www.cncf.io/blog/2023/11/20/announcing-the-platform-engineering-maturity-model/), [CNCF TAG App Delivery whitepaper](https://tag-app-delivery.cncf.io/whitepapers/platform-eng-maturity-model/)). It defines platform engineering as "the practice used to offer an internal platform as a product through investment in all parts of building platforms and their capabilities – the people, processes, policy, and technology which in turn drive business outcomes" ([CNCF blog](https://www.cncf.io/blog/2023/11/20/announcing-the-platform-engineering-maturity-model/)). The model is structured around **5 aspects and 4 levels of maturity**, with each aspect/level combination described through real-world characteristics and scenarios gathered from nearly 50 contributing practitioners across small and large, fast-moving and highly regulated organizations. The CNCF community has since proposed building a formal self-assessment tool on top of the model ([CNCF GitHub issue #1679](https://github.com/cncf/toc/issues/1679)), confirming that automated/semi-automated scoring against this model is an actively discussed but not-yet-shipped capability in the open-source ecosystem.

The five aspects used throughout the CNCF model — and echoed in derivative frameworks — are: **Investment** (staffing/funding allocation to platform capabilities), **Adoption** (how and why users discover/use the platform — extrinsic push vs. intrinsic pull vs. participatory), **Operations** (how platform capabilities are planned, prioritized, developed, and maintained), **Interfaces** (how users interact with and consume platform capabilities, from ad hoc scripts to fully self-service), and **Measurement** (how feedback and learning are gathered and incorporated) ([Puppet/Weave State of Platform Engineering Report Vol. 4, 2025](https://5890440.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/5890440/State%20of%20Platform%20Engineering%20Vol%204%202025.pdf)).

### 1.2 Puppet/Weave "State of Platform Engineering" Report (Volume 4, 2025)

Puppet (via its Weave Intelligence research arm) publishes an annual **State of Platform Engineering Report** that operationalizes the CNCF model with survey data. Volume 4 (2025), sponsored by Broadcom/VMware, surveyed practitioners and found substantial variance in maturity across all five CNCF aspects ([Puppet State of Platform Engineering Vol. 4 PDF](https://5890440.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/5890440/State%20of%20Platform%20Engineering%20Vol%204%202025.pdf)):

- **Adoption**: 36.6% of organizations report adoption is driven by "extrinsic push" (top-down mandate), 28.2% by genuine intrinsic value ("pull"), 18.3% by participatory/community-driven engagement, and 16.9% describe adoption as "erratic" with no clear strategy.
- **Interfaces**: 43.2% rely on "standard tooling" as the primary interface, 31.5% offer true self-service, 15.0% still depend on custom/manual processes, and only 10.3% report fully integrated services embedded seamlessly into existing tools.
- **Operations**: 35.7% report centrally enabled platform work focused on user needs, 31.9% track work centrally but with only partial organization, 18.8% still operate ad hoc/by request, and 13.6% have proactive, fully managed services.
- **Measurement**: 35.2% rely on ad hoc/inconsistent feedback, 20.2% collect feedback consistently but analyze it only lightly, 25.8% combine quantitative and qualitative insight, and only 18.8% achieve comprehensive, fully integrated measurement — with **nearly 30% of platform teams reporting they do not measure platform success at all**, a figure the report calls "alarming" given its implications for proving ROI.

The report's central 2025 finding is that "platform engineering is eating the world," with the discipline absorbing security, observability, data, and FinOps directly into the platform paradigm — a strategy the report calls **"shifting down"** (embedding controls into the platform) as opposed to "shifting left" (merely moving toil earlier in the SDLC). Crucially, **94% of surveyed organizations now see AI as critical to the future of platform engineering**, and the report frames the platform engineer as an active driver — not merely a consumer — of AI within the org ([Puppet State of Platform Engineering Vol. 4 PDF](https://5890440.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/5890440/State%20of%20Platform%20Engineering%20Vol%204%202025.pdf)).

### 1.3 Microsoft's Platform Engineering Capability Model

Microsoft has published its own **Platform Engineering Capability Model**, derived from customer research (survey data plus over 30 in-depth customer interviews) and explicitly designed to closely align with the CNCF model ([Microsoft Learn: Platform Engineering Capability Model](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/platform-engineering/platform-engineering-capability-model)). It identifies **six core capabilities**: investment, adoption, governance, provisioning and management, interfaces, and measurement/feedback — essentially the CNCF's five aspects with "governance" and "provisioning and management" split out as distinct concerns. Microsoft ships this as both a manual self-assessment (a "spider/radar chart" where organizations plot current vs. desired maturity per capability) and a formal online survey instrument, the **Platform Engineering Technical Assessment** ([Microsoft Learn Assessment tool](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/assessments/86bf5228-7b58-4854-b932-ac71402e0be4/)). This is one of the few frameworks in the space that ships an actual interactive self-assessment survey rather than just a conceptual whitepaper.

### 1.4 Team Topologies

**Team Topologies** (Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais) is not a maturity model per se, but a widely adopted organizational design framework that platform-engineering maturity models depend on for defining team structures. It defines four fundamental team types — **stream-aligned**, **platform**, **enabling**, and **complicated-subsystem** teams — and three team interaction modes (collaboration, X-as-a-Service, facilitating) ([Atlassian: Team Topologies](https://www.atlassian.com/devops/frameworks/team-topologies), [Team Topologies key concepts](https://teamtopologies.com/key-concepts)). Team Topologies ships a formal online **Team Topologies Assessment** survey to help organizations self-diagnose "fast flow" readiness ([Team Topologies Assessment](https://survey.teamtopologies.com/tt-assessment), [Team Topologies Assessments page](https://teamtopologies.com/assessments)) and a separate **Team Cognitive Load Assessment** open-source tool on GitHub for measuring whether stream-aligned teams are overloaded — a leading indicator that platform/enabling-team investment is under-resourced ([Team-Cognitive-Load-Assessment GitHub](https://github.com/TeamTopologies/Team-Cognitive-Load-Assessment)). In January 2025, Team Topologies published guidance specifically on how the framework should evolve to accommodate generative AI integration, arguing that AI capability itself increasingly behaves like a "platform" that must be exposed as a well-bounded, team-API-consumable service rather than scattered ad hoc tool access ([Team Topologies: How Team Topologies Can Transform Generative AI Integration](https://teamtopologies.com/news-blogs-newsletters/2025/1/28/how-team-topologies-can-transform-generative-ai-integration)).

### 1.5 DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment) Capabilities Model

DORA, now under Google Cloud, has published the **Accelerate State of DevOps Report** annually for over a decade, identifying capabilities (version control discipline, small batch sizes, deployment automation, etc.) statistically correlated with high organizational performance ([DORA FAQ](https://dora.dev/faq/)). DORA also offers a free self-assessment instrument, the **DORA Quick Check** ([dora.dev/quickcheck](https://dora.dev/quickcheck/)), and publishes its full annual survey instrument publicly, including detailed Likert-scale and multiple-choice questions on deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, and — as of the 2024/2025 surveys — extensive AI-usage questions covering reliance on AI for code review, debugging, test writing, security analysis, and legacy modernization, plus questions on organizational AI stance and approval-process latency ([DORA 2024 Research Questions](https://dora.dev/research/2024/questions/)).

In **2025, DORA published its first "State of AI-assisted Software Development" report**, based on over 100 hours of qualitative research and survey responses from nearly 5,000 technology professionals, in partnership with Google Cloud, GitHub, GitLab, IT Revolution, SkillBench, and Workhelix ([DORA 2025 Report](https://dora.dev/dora-report-2025/), [Google Cloud announcement](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/announcing-the-2025-dora-report), [Google Research abstract](https://research.google/pubs/dora-2025-state-of-ai-assisted-software-development-report/)). Its central finding: **"AI's primary role in software development is that of an amplifier"** — it magnifies the strengths of high-performing organizations and the dysfunctions of struggling ones, meaning AI tooling alone does not fix an immature platform; it *reveals and accelerates* whatever underlying process maturity already exists ([DORA 2025 Report](https://dora.dev/dora-report-2025/)). This report introduces the **DORA AI Capabilities Model**, detailed in Section 2 below, and segments organizations into **seven distinct team profiles** ranging from "harmonious high-achievers" to teams caught in a "legacy bottleneck," explicitly reframing AI maturity as a systems problem rather than a tools problem, with **Value Stream Management (VSM)** proposed as the mechanism that converts local AI productivity gains into measurable organization-wide performance rather than downstream chaos ([Google Cloud: 2025 DORA Report](https://cloud.google.com/resources/content/2025-dora-ai-assisted-software-development-report)).

### 1.6 SPACE Framework

The **SPACE framework** (Satisfaction and well-being, Performance, Activity, Communication and collaboration, Efficiency and flow), introduced by Microsoft Research/GitHub/University of Victoria researchers in the 2021 ACM Queue paper "The SPACE of Developer Productivity," argues that no single metric (like commit count or story points) captures developer productivity — multiple dimensions must be measured together to avoid gaming individual metrics ([ACM Queue: The SPACE of Developer Productivity](https://queue.acm.org/detail.cfm?id=3454124)). SPACE is not itself a maturity model with defined levels but is frequently used as the measurement instrumentation layer inside maturity assessments — i.e., an organization's SPACE-metric coverage (how many of the five dimensions it actually captures, and how) is itself a maturity signal ([DX: What is the SPACE Framework](https://getdx.com/blog/space-metrics/), [Jellyfish: SPACE Framework](https://jellyfish.co/library/space-framework/)). In 2025-2026, Microsoft Research extended this work with **"The SPACE of AI: Real-World Lessons on AI's Impact on Developers,"** applying the SPACE lens specifically to how AI coding tools change developer experience metrics ([Microsoft Research: The SPACE of AI](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/the-space-of-ai-real-world-lessons-on-ais-impact-on-developers/)), and a late-2025 arXiv paper further explored applying SPACE metrics with quantitative instrumentation ([arXiv: Exploring metrics with the SPACE model](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.20955v1)).

### 1.7 Vendor Scorecard/Maturity Tools: Cortex, OpsLevel, Port.io

Three commercial Internal Developer Portal vendors have each built their own maturity frameworks layered on top of (or adjacent to) the CNCF model, and each ships an actual product feature — not just a whitepaper — for scoring maturity:

- **Cortex.io** publishes the **"Cortex Engineering Maturity Curve,"** framing IDP adoption as a progression that starts with basic entity/ownership ingestion, moves through standardization (scorecards enforcing best practices), and ends in fully self-serve, AI-augmented engineering excellence. Cortex explicitly contrasts two IDP design philosophies in market — "self-serve-rooted" IDPs (Backstage-style, built around abstracting infrastructure complexity) versus information-architecture-rooted IDPs (built around cataloging first) — arguing this philosophical split explains much of the confusion orgs face when adopting an IDP ([Cortex: Engineering Maturity Curve](https://www.cortex.io/post/cortex-engineering-maturity-curve)). Cortex's **Scorecards** feature lets organizations define rules against catalog entities, assign point/level values, and track progress over time — effectively a live, queryable maturity-scoring engine ([Cortex Docs: Scorecards](https://docs.cortex.io/standardize/scorecards)).
- **OpsLevel** ships a **Maturity Rubric and Scorecards** product feature that lets platform teams define custom maturity levels per service and automatically evaluate services against defined checks pulled from integrated tools ([OpsLevel: Rubric and Scorecards](https://www.opslevel.com/product/maturity/rubric-and-scorecards)).
- **Port.io** publishes blog content directly mapping the CNCF Platform Engineering Maturity Model to concrete IDP investment decisions, and an annual **State of Internal Developer Portals** report ([Port.io: Using the Platform Engineering Maturity Model to Evaluate Your IDP Investment](https://www.port.io/blog/using-the-platform-engineering-maturity-model-to-evaluate-your-investment-in-internal-developer-platforms), [Port.io: 2024 State of Internal Developer Portal Report](https://www.port.io/blog/the-2024-state-of-internal-developer-portal-report)).

### 1.8 Backstage and Golden Paths

**Backstage**, Spotify's open-source developer portal framework donated to CNCF (now a CNCF Incubation project), remains the reference implementation underlying most IDP maturity thinking. Its core primitives — the **Software Catalog** (a single source of truth for all services, libraries, and infra), **Software Templates** (the mechanism for implementing "golden paths" — standardized, paved-road ways to spin up new services), and **TechDocs** (docs-as-code) — are the concrete technical artifacts that most maturity frameworks are implicitly checking for the presence/absence of ([Backstage: What is Backstage](https://backstage.io/docs/overview/what-is-backstage/)). The presence, breadth, and actual usage rate of Software Templates in a Backstage (or Backstage-like) deployment is one of the most directly *scannable* maturity signals available, since template repositories and their instantiation counts are queryable via the Backstage API and catalog-info.yaml metadata.

## 2. Emerging Maturity Models for Agentic Software Development / AI Coding Agent Adoption

Unlike IDP/platform engineering maturity, **no single, universally recognized "Agentic SDLC Maturity Model" yet exists as of mid-2026** — the space is moving too fast for a canonical model to have crystallized. However, several converging frameworks from different vendors and standards bodies are emerging, each covering a piece of the puzzle.

### 2.1 DORA AI Capabilities Model (2025)

The most authoritative candidate is DORA's **AI Capabilities Model**, introduced as a companion to the 2025 State of AI-assisted Software Development report. It identifies **seven foundational organizational capabilities** that determine whether AI productivity gains scale into organizational performance rather than dissipating into local, ungoverned productivity gains ([Faros AI: Key Takeaways from the DORA Report 2025](https://www.faros.ai/blog/key-takeaways-from-the-dora-report-2025)):

1. **Clear and communicated AI stance** — organizational clarity on what AI tools are permitted and expected
2. **Healthy data ecosystems** — quality, accessible, unified internal data
3. **AI-accessible internal data** — context integration beyond generic/ungrounded AI assistance
4. **Strong version control practices** — mature development workflow and rollback capability
5. **Working in small batches** — maintaining incremental-change discipline even as AI accelerates output
6. **User-centric focus** — product strategy clarity despite accelerated velocity
7. **Quality internal platforms** — the technical foundation (i.e., IDP maturity) that enables AI gains to scale

This is the most important finding for this research task: **DORA explicitly ties agentic/AI maturity to platform engineering maturity** — "quality internal platforms" is listed as one of the seven capabilities, meaning IDP maturity is treated as a *prerequisite input* to ADP maturity, not a separate axis. This directly validates the premise of a combined IDP+ADP maturity assessment.

### 2.2 Cortex "AI Readiness" Scorecard (Bronze/Silver/Gold)

Cortex ships a concrete, three-tier **AI Readiness** scorecard template — the closest thing in the current commercial market to a shippable, automatable ADP maturity rubric ([Cortex Docs: AI Readiness](https://docs.cortex.io/solutions/ai-readiness)):

- **Bronze**: Service ownership defined, runbook linked, on-call rotation configured
- **Silver**: SLOs defined, CI/CD pipeline active, dependency inventory current
- **Gold**: AI model security scanning enabled, test coverage minimum met, DORA metrics (deployment frequency, MTTR) within target benchmarks

Cortex also ships a companion **"AI Maturity"** configuration workflow (distinct from "AI Readiness") focused specifically on tracking AI *tool usage* impact: ingest entities and establish ownership, configure a Scorecard to enforce AI-tooling standards, automate AI tooling requests via Workflows and Cortex's own MCP server, and review Engineering Intelligence metrics for trends in AI tool usage across PR size, review time, and merge frequency ([Cortex Docs: Configure for AI Maturity](https://docs.cortex.io/solutions/ai-maturity/configure)). This is a directly operational precedent for the type of automated scoring system this research task is investigating.

### 2.3 CSA MCP Security Maturity Model

The **Cloud Security Alliance (CSA)** published, in a March 2026 whitepaper, an **"Agentic MCP Security Best Practices Guide"** containing a formal **four-level MCP Security Maturity Model** — the most rigorous and specific agent-governance maturity framework identified in this research ([CSA: Agentic MCP Security Best Practices Guide](https://labs.cloudsecurityalliance.org/agentic/agentic-mcp-security-best-practices-v1/)):

- **Level 1 — Basic Authentication and Transport Security**: All MCP connections authenticated (OAuth 2.1 + PKCE for remote servers per the November 2025 MCP spec); TLS 1.2+ mandatory; complete MCP server inventory maintained (name, version, deployment location, business owner); least-privilege service accounts (no broad personal access tokens); basic audit logging with 90-day retention; documented quarterly vulnerability review process.
- **Level 2 — Tool Integrity Verification and Session Hardening**: Tool description/schema hashing and change detection between sessions (via client-side hashing, a tool registry, or open-source scanners like `mcp-scan`); access tokens capped at 1-hour lifetime with refresh rotation; no plaintext token storage; automated CVE remediation workflow with a maximum 30-day window for High/Critical severities.
- **Level 3 and 4** (execution isolation, behavioral/runtime monitoring, and supply-chain validation) extend the model further, explicitly cross-referenced against the **OWASP Agentic Security Initiative (ASI)** risk taxonomy, the **CSA AI Controls Matrix (AICM)**, and **MITRE ATLAS** adversarial ML techniques.

This maturity model is grounded in real incident data: the CSA reports **over 30 MCP-related CVEs filed between January and February 2026 alone**, including CVE-2025-6514 (CVSS 9.6) affecting the widely used `mcp-remote` proxy package across more than 437,000 installations, cross-tenant data exposure at Asana, prompt-injection attacks against the GitHub MCP server, and unauthenticated RCE in Anthropic's own MCP Inspector tool. By early 2026, security researchers had catalogued nearly 7,000 internet-exposed MCP servers, roughly half with no authentication whatsoever ([CSA MCP Security Best Practices](https://labs.cloudsecurityalliance.org/agentic/agentic-mcp-security-best-practices-v1/)). This gives the maturity model unusually strong empirical grounding compared to most agentic-AI frameworks, which remain largely aspirational.

### 2.4 Repository/Codebase "Agent Readiness" Assessment Skills

A grassroots pattern has emerged of open-source "skills" (structured prompt/tooling packages for agentic coding assistants like Claude Code) that assess a given code repository's readiness for autonomous agent workflows. One example found in this research is a published skill that **analyzes 81 criteria across 8 pillars and assigns a 1-5 maturity level with a generated visual report** ("Repository Readiness Assessment," designed to "audit any repository to determine readiness for autonomous AI agent workflows") ([NeverSight skills_feed: readiness skill](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/NeverSight/skills_feed/refs/heads/main/data/skills-md/viktor-silakov/readiness/readiness/SKILL.md)). Similar tools exist for turning "an unfamiliar repository into a concrete engineering assessment" by identifying structure, run/build mechanics, and risk areas ([OpenClaw AI: Repo Analysis skill](https://openclawai.io/skills/skill/repo-analysis)), and blog-level guidance exists asking "Is My Code Repo AI Ready?" focused on whether an agent landing cold in a codebase can understand how it works without loading the entire repo into context ([IBERANT: Is My Code Repo AI Ready?](https://www.iberant.es/eu/blog/is-my-code-repo-ai-ready)). These are early, decentralized, community-driven precedents for exactly the kind of automated repo-level ADP-readiness scanning this research task is investigating, though none has achieved the standardization or vendor backing of the CNCF/DORA models.

### 2.5 AGENT.md / AGENTS.md — An Emerging Metadata Standard

A concrete, rapidly adopted standard has emerged for one specific ADP signal: agent configuration metadata. **AGENT.md**, authored by Geoffrey Huntley at Sourcegraph (July 2025), proposes a single standardized configuration file format that lets a codebase "speak directly to any agentic coding tool," replacing the previous fragmentation of tool-specific files (`.cursorrules`, `.windsurfrules`, `.clauderules`, GitHub Copilot instructions, etc.) ([AGENT.md GitHub](https://github.com/agentmd/agent.md)). A closely related community convention, **AGENTS.md**, has emerged as what multiple 2025-2026 sources call "the emerging open standard for AI coding agents" ([Remio: What is Agents.md](https://www.remio.ai/post/what-is-agents-md-a-complete-guide-to-the-new-ai-coding-agent-standard-in-2025), [Atlan: How to Write an AGENTS.md File](https://atlan.com/know/how-to-write-agents-md/)). **The presence, completeness, and freshness of an AGENT.md/AGENTS.md file (or its predecessor tool-specific equivalents) is one of the single most directly scannable, binary-detectable ADP maturity signals available today** — a repository either has one or it doesn't, and its content can be parsed for structured completeness (build commands, test commands, code style, PR conventions).

### 2.6 Vendor Landscape: Microsoft, GitHub, Atlassian, Harness

- **Microsoft** frames agentic maturity through the lens of Azure AI Foundry and its "agent factory" positioning, plus Microsoft Entra's evolving guidance on non-human identity and access control for AI agents, treating agent RBAC/identity as core infrastructure rather than a bolt-on ([Microsoft Tech Community: AI agents are everywhere. Are your access controls ready?](https://techcommunity.microsoft.com/blog/microsoft-entra-blog/ai-agents-are-everywhere-are-your-access-controls-ready/4531379)).
- **GitHub** has not published a formal maturity model but has published extensive best-practice guidance treating AI coding agents as "powerful teammates, not autonomous committers," recommending guardrails such as draft-PR-only agent workflows, no direct-to-main commits, and observable/auditable agent action logs — de facto governance maturity signals ([GitHub Community: Best practices for using GitHub AI coding agents in production](https://github.com/orgs/community/discussions/182197)). GitHub also documents secret-scanning integration specifically for the GitHub MCP server, indicating built-in product support for one Level-1/2 MCP security control ([GitHub Docs: Scanning for secrets with the GitHub MCP server](https://docs.github.com/enterprise-cloud@latest/code-security/how-tos/use-ghas-with-ai-coding-agents/scan-for-secrets-with-github-mcp-server)).
- **Atlassian** frames maturity primarily through the Team Topologies lens applied to DevOps/AI-augmented delivery rather than a standalone agentic model ([Atlassian: Team Topologies](https://www.atlassian.com/devops/frameworks/team-topologies)).
- **Harness**, **Cortex**, **Port.io**, **Humanitec**, and **OpsLevel** are all iterating their existing IDP scorecard products to add AI-specific rule templates (as documented above for Cortex), rather than shipping wholly separate "ADP" products — reinforcing that in practice, the market is converging on **extending IDP maturity tooling to cover ADP signals**, not building parallel infrastructure.

## 3. How AI/LLM Tools Can Perform Automated Discovery and Scanning

Automated discovery of IDP/ADP maturity is technically achievable today through a layered combination of **static repository analysis**, **API/telemetry-based runtime discovery**, and **LLM-based summarization/classification** — and partial implementations of each layer already exist in production tools.

### 3.1 Static Repository Analysis (Source Code + Config Scanning)

The most tractable and privacy-safe discovery layer is scanning version-controlled artifacts for the *presence and structure* of maturity-relevant files:

- **CI/CD configuration**: presence/complexity of `.github/workflows/`, `.gitlab-ci.yml`, Azure Pipelines YAML, Jenkinsfiles — presence indicates automation maturity; multi-stage pipelines with automated testing/security gates indicate higher maturity than single-stage build-only pipelines.
- **Infrastructure as Code (IaC)**: presence and coverage ratio of Terraform/CDKTF/Pulumi/Helm charts/Kubernetes manifests relative to total services in the catalog — a service with zero IaC files but a running production deployment is a strong "shadow infrastructure" signal indicating low IDP maturity.
- **Service catalog artifacts**: presence of `catalog-info.yaml` (Backstage), `.cortex.yaml` (Cortex), or `opslevel.yml` files, and whether the metadata fields (owner, tier, on-call, SLOs) are populated versus left as template defaults.
- **Golden-path/template usage**: for Backstage specifically, the ratio of services created via **Software Templates** versus manually onboarded — queryable via the Backstage Scaffolder API and catalog provenance annotations ([Backstage overview](https://backstage.io/docs/overview/what-is-backstage/)).
- **AI agent configuration files**: presence of `.claude/`, `.cursor/`, `.cursorrules`, `.github/copilot-instructions.md`, `AGENT.md`/`AGENTS.md`, and MCP server config files (e.g., `.mcp.json`, `mcp_config.json`). The mere presence of these files is a Level-0-to-1 ADP signal; their content quality (specificity of build/test commands, documented conventions) and last-modified recency are higher-order signals.
- **Cloud/on-prem signals**: provider-specific resource definitions in IaC (`aws_*`, `azurerm_*`, `google_*` Terraform resource types; vSphere/VMware Cloud Foundation manifests for on-prem/private-cloud deployments) reveal single-cloud vs. multi-cloud vs. on-prem/hybrid posture directly from code, without needing runtime API access.

An emerging category of tools already performs this specific check as a discrete product: **MCP configuration scanners** such as `mcp-scan` (referenced directly in the CSA maturity model as a Level-2 control-implementation option) ([CSA MCP Security Best Practices](https://labs.cloudsecurityalliance.org/agentic/agentic-mcp-security-best-practices-v1/), [Reddit: I built mcp-scan](https://www.reddit.com/r/ClaudeAI/comments/1s2qyp2/i_built_mcpscan_a_security_scanner_for_your_mcp/)), **APIsec MCP Discovery and Audit** ([APIsec MCP Audit](https://apisec-inc.github.io/mcp-audit/)), and Apify's **MCP Server Discovery & Quality Scanner** ([Apify MCP Scanner](https://apify.com/express_kingfisher/mcp-server-discovery-scanner)).

### 3.2 Runtime/API-Based Discovery

Static analysis alone cannot detect actual usage or runtime posture. Complementary API-driven discovery includes:

- **GitHub/GitLab/Azure DevOps APIs**: organization-wide repository metadata, branch protection rules, required-review counts, PR merge/review latency, Actions/Pipeline run history and success rates, and (per GitHub's own DORA partnership) direct DORA metric computation from commit and deployment event history ([DORA 2025 Report research partners](https://dora.dev/dora-report-2025/)).
- **Cloud provider APIs** (AWS, Azure, GCP): actual deployed resource inventories, which can be cross-referenced against IaC state files to compute a **"drift ratio"** — the percentage of live infrastructure *not* represented in code — a direct, quantifiable IaC-coverage maturity metric.
- **Kubernetes API**: cluster inventories, namespace/RBAC policy counts, GPU node pool presence (a direct AI-infrastructure-readiness signal for on-prem/hybrid GPU clusters), and Custom Resource Definitions indicating platform-team-built abstractions (Crossplane compositions, Argo Rollouts, etc.).
- **MCP server registries/gateways**: enterprise MCP gateway products (e.g., the open-source **MCP Gateway & Registry** project) provide a live inventory of which MCP servers are registered, their authentication configuration, and usage volume — directly operationalizing Level-1 of the CSA maturity model ("maintain an inventory of all MCP servers in use") ([MCP Gateway & Registry GitHub](https://ithub.global.ssl.fastly.net/agentic-community/mcp-gateway-registry)).
- **Engineering intelligence platforms** (see Section 5) already perform this cross-source API aggregation commercially and could serve as a data layer beneath a maturity-scoring agent rather than requiring the scoring agent to build all integrations from scratch.

### 3.3 LLM-Based Summarization and Classification

Raw scan output (thousands of files, API responses) is not directly a maturity score — an LLM layer is needed to classify and summarize signals into rubric-aligned judgments. This is precisely the approach used by the community "repo readiness" skills identified in Section 2.4: an agent reads a repository's structure, README, CI config, and code samples, then produces a structured maturity judgment against a fixed rubric (e.g., "81 criteria across 8 pillars," 1-5 scale) rather than a free-form summary ([NeverSight readiness skill](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/NeverSight/skills_feed/refs/heads/main/data/skills-md/viktor-silakov/readiness/readiness/SKILL.md)). LLM summarization is also the natural mechanism for qualitative document review — e.g., grading the specificity and freshness of runbooks, ADRs (architecture decision records), and onboarding docs, none of which can be scored by simple presence/absence checks alone.

### 3.4 Surveys/Interviews vs. Automated Telemetry

The Puppet/Weave and CNCF research programs rely primarily on **self-reported survey data** (Likert-scale and multiple-choice questions administered to practitioners) rather than automated telemetry ([Puppet State of Platform Engineering Vol. 4](https://5890440.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/5890440/State%20of%20Platform%20Engineering%20Vol%204%202025.pdf), [DORA 2024 Research Questions](https://dora.dev/research/2024/questions/)). This reflects a real limitation: surveys capture *intent, culture, and perceived experience* that code/API scanning cannot see (e.g., "why" adoption happened, whether a mandate felt coercive, whether measurement practice is trusted by the team) — but they are subject to self-report bias, low response rates, and staleness the moment they are collected. Automated telemetry-based approaches (static repo analysis + API discovery) are objective and continuously refreshable but blind to intent, politics, and qualitative experience, and can be fooled by "maturity theater" (e.g., a populated `catalog-info.yaml` that nobody actually consults). **The strongest available approach — validated by every major vendor examined, including Microsoft's dual manual-chart-plus-survey assessment and Cortex's scorecard-plus-Engineering-Intelligence combination — is hybrid: automated scanning for objective, continuously-verifiable signals, supplemented by targeted survey/interview instruments for intent, culture, and gap-filling** ([Microsoft: Platform Engineering Capability Model](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/platform-engineering/platform-engineering-capability-model), [Cortex: AI Readiness](https://docs.cortex.io/solutions/ai-readiness)). Section 7 and Section 8 of this report expand this hybrid approach in depth.

### 4.2 Proposed Synthesized Rubric

The proposed rubric below unifies IDP and ADP maturity into a single five-tier model (Level 0 through Level 4), with concrete, scannable signals per dimension per tier. Tier labels follow the CNCF/CMMI convention (Ad-hoc → Emerging → Defined → Managed → Optimized) used throughout the source frameworks.

| Dimension | Level 0 — Ad-hoc | Level 1 — Emerging | Level 2 — Defined | Level 3 — Managed | Level 4 — Optimized |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Infrastructure & cloud posture** | No IaC; manual console changes; unknown on-prem/cloud mix; no GPU inventory | Partial IaC (<30% coverage); single cloud provider adopted informally; GPU/AI infra ad hoc, unbudgeted | IaC covers majority of production (>60%); explicit single- or multi-cloud strategy documented; dedicated GPU pool or committed cloud AI-compute budget exists | Near-complete IaC (>90%) with drift detection; multi-cloud/hybrid strategy with documented failover; GPU capacity centrally managed via platform (e.g., Kubernetes GPU operator, quota system) | IaC coverage >98% with automated drift remediation; cloud/on-prem placement optimized per workload via policy engine; AI/GPU infra is a first-class, self-service platform capability (per [Puppet Vol. 4 Reference Architecture for Data/AI IDP on GCP](https://5890440.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/5890440/State%20of%20Platform%20Engineering%20Vol%204%202025.pdf)) |
| **Developer productivity metrics** | No metrics captured; success unmeasured (≈30% of orgs per [Puppet Vol. 4](https://5890440.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/5890440/State%20of%20Platform%20Engineering%20Vol%204%202025.pdf)) | Ad hoc, inconsistent feedback collection; some DORA metrics tracked manually | Consistent DORA metric capture (deployment frequency, lead time, MTTR, change failure rate) via dashboard tool; SPACE dimensions partially instrumented | DORA + SPACE metrics both instrumented via platform (LinearB/Jellyfish/Swarmia/Faros/Code Climate Velocity or equivalent); combined quant + qual feedback loops ([Jellyfish Data Hub](https://jellyfish.co/platform/data-hub/)) | Metrics feed automated Value Stream Management and continuous improvement loops; AI-usage impact on DORA/SPACE metrics explicitly tracked per [DORA 2025 AI Capabilities Model](https://dora.dev/dora-report-2025/) |
| **Service catalog / self-service** | No catalog; tribal knowledge only | Catalog exists but sparsely populated / stale metadata; templates absent | Catalog covers most services with owner/tier metadata; basic golden-path templates exist (Backstage Software Templates or equivalent) | Catalog is source of truth, auto-populated via CI integration; broad template library covers most new-service creation; self-service provisioning for infra requests | Catalog drives automated scorecards/compliance; golden paths cover >90% of new service creation; self-service extends to AI/agent resource provisioning |
| **AI agent governance, security, RBAC** | No AI agent usage policy; unauthenticated/ungoverned MCP or agent tool access; no inventory | Informal AI tool usage; CSA MCP Security **Level 1** controls partial (some auth, no full inventory) | CSA MCP Security **Level 1** complete (OAuth 2.1/PKCE, TLS 1.2+, full server inventory, least-privilege service accounts, 90-day audit logs); documented AI usage policy ([CSA](https://labs.cloudsecurityalliance.org/agentic/agentic-mcp-security-best-practices-v1/)) | CSA **Level 2** complete (tool integrity verification/hashing, session hardening, <30-day CVE remediation SLA); agent RBAC modeled beyond static roles (contextual/attribute-based authorization per [tianpan.co: RBAC Is Not Enough for AI Agents](https://tianpan.co/blog/2026-04-20-rbac-ai-agents-authorization)) | CSA **Level 3-4** complete (execution isolation, behavioral/runtime monitoring, supply-chain validation); agent actions fully auditable and mapped to OWASP ASI/MITRE ATLAS risk categories |
| **Agent skill/tool discovery & reuse** | No shared agent config; every developer/team reinvents prompts and tool configs | Scattered tool-specific config files (`.cursorrules`, `.clauderules`) with no standardization | Unified `AGENT.md`/`AGENTS.md` present in most repos ([AGENT.md](https://github.com/agentmd/agent.md)); informal MCP server list maintained | Centralized MCP gateway/registry deployed with authenticated, versioned server catalog ([MCP Gateway & Registry](https://ithub.global.ssl.fastly.net/agentic-community/mcp-gateway-registry)); agent skills/prompts versioned and reused across teams | Enterprise-wide agent skill marketplace/registry with usage analytics, approval workflow, and automatic propagation of updated skills across repos |
| **Culture/process (golden paths, platform team structure, Team Topologies alignment)** | No dedicated platform team; every team owns its own infra/tooling | Informal platform function exists but lacks product-management discipline; adoption driven by mandate only | Dedicated platform team operates as a Team-Topologies "platform team" offering clear X-as-a-Service interfaces; golden paths documented | Platform treated explicitly as a product with a roadmap, feedback loops, and intrinsic-value-driven adoption (>40% "pull" adoption per Puppet benchmarks); enabling teams support stream-aligned team upskilling | Platform organization fully aligned to Team Topologies with measured cognitive-load balancing ([Team Cognitive Load Assessment](https://github.com/TeamTopologies/Team-Cognitive-Load-Assessment)); AI capability itself is treated as a platform-exposed service per [Team Topologies GenAI guidance](https://teamtopologies.com/news-blogs-newsletters/2025/1/28/how-team-topologies-can-transform-generative-ai-integration) |

This rubric intentionally treats IDP and ADP maturity as **interlocking, not independent**, dimensions — consistent with DORA's explicit finding that "quality internal platforms" is one of the seven capabilities that determines whether AI investment pays off ([DORA 2025 AI Capabilities Model via Faros AI](https://www.faros.ai/blog/key-takeaways-from-the-dora-report-2025)). An organization cannot reach Level 3-4 on the ADP-specific rows without first having reached at least Level 2 on the IDP rows, because agent governance and self-service require the catalog/ownership/CI foundation that IDP maturity provides.

## 5. Existing Vendor Products and Open-Source Tools for Automated Maturity Scanning

No vendor currently ships a single unified "IDP+ADP maturity scanner," but multiple product categories already implement large pieces of the required functionality:

- **Cortex.io Scorecards** — the most directly applicable commercial precedent. Scorecards let organizations define rules against catalog entities pulling data from third-party integrations, assign point/level values, and gamify progress; Cortex explicitly ships pre-built **AI Readiness** (Bronze/Silver/Gold) and **AI Maturity** scorecard templates as documented in Section 2.2 ([Cortex Docs: Scorecards](https://docs.cortex.io/standardize/scorecards), [Cortex: Scorecard Examples](https://docs.cortex.io/standardize/scorecards/scorecard-examples)).
- **OpsLevel Maturity Rubric and Scorecards** — lets platform teams define arbitrary maturity levels per service, evaluated automatically against integrated data sources, with a **Service Maturity Report** view for tracking progress over time ([OpsLevel: Rubric and Scorecards](https://www.opslevel.com/product/maturity/rubric-and-scorecards)).
- **Port.io** — publishes maturity-model-aligned content and blog guidance mapping CNCF's model to concrete IDP feature investment, though its scoring capability is delivered through its broader self-service/catalog product rather than a named "maturity" feature ([Port.io: Platform Engineering Maturity Model blog](https://www.port.io/blog/using-the-platform-engineering-maturity-model-to-evaluate-your-investment-in-internal-developer-platforms)).
- **Engineering intelligence / DORA-metric platforms** — **LinearB**, **Jellyfish**, **Swarmia**, **Faros AI**, **Code Climate Velocity**, and **GitClear** all ingest Git/CI/ticketing data via API to compute DORA and SPACE-style metrics automatically, which is the measurement layer that maturity scoring depends on. Jellyfish specifically markets a **"Data Hub"** for unifying engineering signal sources ([Jellyfish Data Hub](https://jellyfish.co/platform/data-hub/)) and a dedicated **Platform Engineering** solution ([Jellyfish for Platform Engineering](https://jellyfish.co/solutions/platform-engineering/)); Faros AI has published its own detailed analysis of the DORA 2025 report cross-referenced against its own telemetry findings, indicating an existing feedback loop between vendor telemetry and DORA's framework ([Faros AI: Key Takeaways from DORA Report 2025](https://www.faros.ai/blog/key-takeaways-from-the-dora-report-2025)); GitClear focuses specifically on AI-coding-tool ROI measurement via pull-request-level code analysis ([GitClear: Measure AI ROI](https://www.gitclear.com/)).
- **MCP-specific security/discovery scanners** — `mcp-scan` (open-source, referenced by CSA as a Level-2 control mechanism), **APIsec MCP Discovery and Audit**, and **Apify's MCP Server Discovery & Quality Scanner** represent an emerging category focused narrowly on discovering and grading MCP server security posture across an organization's environment ([mcp-scan via Reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/ClaudeAI/comments/1s2qyp2/i_built_mcpscan_a_security_scanner_for_your_mcp/), [APIsec MCP Audit](https://apisec-inc.github.io/mcp-audit/), [Apify MCP Scanner](https://apify.com/express_kingfisher/mcp-server-discovery-scanner)).
- **MCP Gateway & Registry** (open-source, agentic-community project) — provides enterprise-ready centralized registration, discovery, and governance for MCP servers, directly operationalizing the "maintain complete inventory" requirement of the CSA maturity model's Level 1 ([MCP Gateway & Registry GitHub](https://ithub.global.ssl.fastly.net/agentic-community/mcp-gateway-registry)).
- **Community "repo readiness" assessment skills** — decentralized, agent-native tools (structured Claude Code/agentic skills) that directly compute a repository-level maturity score for AI-agent-workflow readiness, e.g., the 81-criteria/8-pillar skill referenced in Section 2.4 ([NeverSight readiness skill](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/NeverSight/skills_feed/refs/heads/main/data/skills-md/viktor-silakov/readiness/readiness/SKILL.md)).
- **Team Topologies' own assessment tools** — the formal **Team Topologies Assessment** survey and the open-source **Team Cognitive Load Assessment** on GitHub are the clearest existing precedent for a lightweight, standardized org-structure maturity self-assessment that could be embedded inside a broader automated discovery workflow ([Team Topologies Assessment](https://survey.teamtopologies.com/tt-assessment), [Team Cognitive Load Assessment](https://github.com/TeamTopologies/Team-Cognitive-Load-Assessment)).

**Gap identified**: none of the above tools combine *cross-cloud infrastructure scanning*, *repo-level AI-agent-config scanning*, *engineering-metrics ingestion*, and *survey-based culture validation* into a single unified IDP+ADP maturity score. This represents a genuine white-space opportunity, discussed further in Section 6.

## 6. Practical Architecture Recommendations for Automated Discovery and Scoring

### 6.1 Proposed Multi-Agent Architecture

An AI-driven maturity discovery system should be architected as a **pipeline of specialized scanning agents feeding a central scoring/reasoning layer**, rather than one monolithic agent, because the data sources (Git APIs, cloud APIs, Kubernetes APIs, document repositories) have fundamentally different authentication models, rate limits, and output shapes:

1. **Connector/Ingestion Layer** — dedicated, narrowly scoped connectors for each source system: GitHub/GitLab/Azure DevOps REST/GraphQL APIs (repos, PRs, Actions/Pipelines, branch protection), cloud provider APIs (AWS/Azure/GCP resource inventories and cost/usage APIs), Kubernetes API server (cluster/namespace/CRD/GPU node inventories), and MCP registry/gateway APIs where deployed. This mirrors exactly the integration architecture Cortex recommends for driving AI maturity — connect version control, project management, code quality/security, and documentation systems as the very first step before any scoring occurs ([Cortex Docs: AI Readiness, Step 1](https://docs.cortex.io/solutions/ai-readiness)).
2. **Static Analysis Layer** — a repository-crawling agent that clones/reads repo trees (without necessarily executing code) to detect the presence, structure, and content quality of IaC files, CI/CD configs, catalog metadata files, and AI agent config files (`AGENT.md`/`AGENTS.md`, `.cursor/`, `.claude/`, `.github/copilot-instructions.md`, MCP config files). This layer should run LLM-based content classification (not just presence/absence regex matching) to grade *quality*, e.g., whether an `AGENT.md` file actually documents build/test commands versus being an empty template.
3. **Cross-Reference / Drift Detection Layer** — reconciles static IaC declarations against live cloud/Kubernetes API inventories to compute drift ratios, unowned-resource counts, and shadow-IT signals — a purely computational step, not requiring an LLM.
4. **Metrics Aggregation Layer** — either integrates directly with existing engineering-intelligence platforms (LinearB, Jellyfish, Faros AI, etc.) via their APIs, or computes DORA/SPACE proxy metrics directly from raw Git/CI event history if no such platform is already deployed. This avoids re-building metric computation that mature commercial tools already do well.
5. **LLM Reasoning/Scoring Layer** — a rubric-grounded LLM agent (using the Section 4 rubric as a structured scoring prompt/schema) ingests the outputs of layers 2-4 per organizational unit (team, service, or business unit) and produces a structured maturity score per dimension, with cited evidence (which file, which API response, which metric value) backing each score — critical for auditability and stakeholder trust, mirroring how the community "repo readiness" skills already generate visual, criteria-cited reports rather than opaque single numbers ([NeverSight readiness skill](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/NeverSight/skills_feed/refs/heads/main/data/skills-md/viktor-silakov/readiness/readiness/SKILL.md)).
6. **Human-in-the-Loop Validation Layer** — surfaces the automated score plus a targeted subset of the Section 8 survey questions to close gaps the automated layers cannot see (see Section 7 for the full rationale).

### 6.2 Feedback and Recommendation Loop

The scoring output should not be a static report but should drive an **iterative recommendation loop** analogous to how Cortex's AI Maturity workflow is structured (ingest → standardize via Scorecards → streamline via automated Workflows → improve via Engineering Intelligence review) ([Cortex Docs: AI Maturity](https://docs.cortex.io/solutions/ai-maturity/configure)):

- **Gap-to-action mapping**: each unmet rubric criterion (e.g., "no IaC drift detection," "no AGENT.md present," "no MCP server inventory") should map to a specific, actionable remediation — ideally an auto-generated pull request or Backstage Software Template invocation rather than just a text recommendation, closing the loop from *diagnosis* to *remediation* automatically where safe to do so (e.g., an agent could open a draft PR adding a baseline `AGENT.md` template to a repo that lacks one, following GitHub's own guidance that AI agents should "propose code, never own it" — draft PRs only, no direct commits) ([GitHub Community: Best practices for AI coding agents in production](https://github.com/orgs/community/discussions/182197)).
- **Re-scan cadence**: because both IDP and especially ADP maturity are moving targets (new MCP CVEs, new agent tooling releases monthly), the scanning pipeline should run on a continuous or at-minimum weekly cadence for the static/API layers, with the human-survey layer refreshed quarterly — mirroring DORA's own annual-plus-continuous-quick-check cadence ([DORA Quick Check](https://dora.dev/quickcheck/)).
- **Benchmarking**: scores should be contextualized against industry benchmarks from the Puppet/Weave, DORA, and CNCF survey datasets (e.g., "your Measurement dimension is at Level 1; industry median per the 2025 State of Platform Engineering Report is Level 2, with 30% of orgs still at Level 0") to make the output actionable rather than abstract ([Puppet Vol. 4](https://5890440.fs1.hubspotusercontent-eu1.net/hubfs/5890440/State%20of%20Platform%20Engineering%20Vol%204%202025.pdf)).

## 7. Scanning Unstructured Organizational Signals: Email, Meeting Transcripts, and SDLC Systems

Automated code/API scanning (Sections 3 and 6) captures *what exists*, but misses *why it exists, how people actually experience it, and what's currently being debated but not yet reflected in code*. A growing set of AI tools now mine exactly this class of unstructured organizational data, and several of their techniques transfer directly to IDP/ADP maturity discovery.

### 7.1 Mining Communication and Process Data for Maturity Signals

**Meeting transcripts.** AI meeting-assistant tools (Gong, Fireflies.ai, Otter.ai, Fathom, Read AI, and increasingly built-in assistants inside Slack and Zoom) already transcribe and summarize recurring engineering meetings, extracting action items, decisions, and follow-ups automatically ([Slack: Best AI Meeting Notetakers for 2026](https://slack.com/blog/productivity/ai-meeting-note-taker-how-it-works-and-features-to-look-for); [V7 Labs: AI Action Item Extraction Agent](https://www.v7labs.com/agents/ai-action-item-extraction-agent); comparison coverage at [Equal Time: Best AI Notetakers 2025](https://equaltime.io/2025/06/03/best-ai-notetakers-in-2025-equal-time-vs-otter-fathom-gong-read-ai-and-fireflies/)). Applied to platform-review boards, architecture review meetings, or AI-governance committee meetings, the same extraction pipeline can surface maturity-relevant signals that never appear in code:
- **Frequency and specificity of "toil"/manual-process complaints** — recurring phrases like "we're still doing this by hand," "nobody owns this," or "we always forget to update the catalog" are direct proxies for the CNCF "Operations" and "Measurement" aspects being at Level 0-1.
- **Named tools and cloud providers mentioned in passing** — engineers discussing "our Azure bill" or "since we moved this to GCP" in a meeting often reveal infrastructure decisions and multi-cloud sprawl before it is ever reflected in a central IaC repo.
- **Decision-making patterns** — whether architecture decisions are made collaboratively in a documented review (a Team Topologies "platform review board" ritual) versus unilaterally and undocumented is a direct culture/process maturity signal.
- **Sentiment and escalation patterns around AI tooling** — repeated mentions of blocked MCP server requests, "waiting on security review for Cursor," or enthusiasm/friction around Copilot/Claude Code rollout are leading indicators of ADP governance maturity (or its absence) well before formal policy catches up.

**Email and Slack/Teams chat history.** Approval-workflow threads (e.g., a chain of emails escalating a production incident, or a Slack thread negotiating a new cloud vendor contract) reveal governance maturity signals: how many approval hops a change requires, whether incident escalation follows a defined runbook or is ad hoc "@here" panic, and whether procurement/vendor decisions (which directly shape the "Infrastructure & cloud posture" rubric dimension) are documented anywhere durable. Slack itself now markets first-party AI agent access to "your organization's public conversation data... across channels, threads, canvases and conversations" specifically to make enterprise agents "more contextually relevant" ([Slack AI Agents](https://slack.com/ai-agents)), confirming that this class of signal is already being operationalized by major platforms, not just hypothetical.

**SDLC systems (Jira/Linear/Azure Boards, PR comments, postmortems, runbooks, on-call data).** These systems are semi-structured and yield some of the highest-confidence soft signals:
- **Cycle time and rework rate** computed from ticket state-transition timestamps are direct DORA/SPACE proxy metrics, extractable without any dedicated engineering-intelligence platform.
- **Ticket categories/labels tied to platform or infra work** (e.g., a persistently large backlog of "request: new golden-path template" or "request: onboard service to catalog" tickets) is a direct, quantifiable measure of unmet self-service demand — arguably a stronger maturity signal than catalog population itself, since it reveals *demand exceeding supply*.
- **Incident postmortems/retros** frequently contain explicit root-cause language ("no owner was paged," "runbook was out of date," "we didn't know this service depended on X") that maps directly onto the CNCF/Cortex governance and ownership criteria.
- **PR/code review comments** mentioning AI-assistant output (e.g., reviewers flagging "this looks AI-generated, please add tests") are an emerging, if noisy, signal of both AI-tool adoption rate and the maturity of review guardrails around it.

**Engineering intelligence platforms already doing pieces of this.** Jellyfish's **Data Hub** explicitly unifies signals from Jira, Slack, and Git into a single engineering-intelligence layer, going beyond pure code metrics into process and collaboration data ([Jellyfish Data Hub](https://jellyfish.co/platform/data-hub/), [Jellyfish Jira integration](https://jellyfish.co/integration/jira/)). GitClear focuses specifically on pull-request-level analysis to measure AI coding tool ROI and code quality impact, effectively mining PR diffs and review metadata as a maturity/impact signal ([GitClear: Measure AI ROI](https://www.gitclear.com/)). Faros AI similarly cross-references its own telemetry against DORA's 2025 findings, showing convergent validation between vendor-collected process signals and academic/industry survey data ([Faros AI: Key Takeaways from DORA Report 2025](https://www.faros.ai/blog/key-takeaways-from-the-dora-report-2025)).

### 7.2 Privacy, Consent, and Data Governance Considerations

Passive organizational mining of this kind sits directly on top of employee-monitoring and workplace-surveillance law, and must be designed accordingly:

- **PII handling and aggregation-only reporting.** The safest architectural default is to extract and report only **aggregated, team-level or org-level signals** (e.g., "23% of platform-review-board meetings this quarter referenced manual catalog updates") rather than individually attributable statements. Worklytics, a vendor purpose-built for privacy-safe productivity analytics, explicitly frames its architecture around this principle — "track productivity without employee surveillance" via a GDPR-compliant framework that aggregates and de-identifies before any human sees the data ([Worklytics: Track Productivity Without Employee Surveillance](https://www.worklytics.co/resources/track-productivity-without-employee-surveillance-gdpr-compliant-framework-2025)).
- **GDPR and lawful basis.** Under EU/UK GDPR, monitoring communications or meeting content generally requires a valid lawful basis (rarely pure consent, given the power imbalance in an employment relationship — consent is often not considered "freely given" in that context) and must satisfy necessity and proportionality tests; the UK ICO's guidance on monitoring workers explicitly requires organizations to conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) before deploying monitoring of this kind ([ICO: Employment practices and data protection — monitoring workers](https://ico.org.uk/media/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/employment-information/employment-practices-and-data-protection-monitoring-workers-1-0.pdf)). A detailed compliance walkthrough of GDPR applied specifically to workplace analytics tooling is provided by Gable, covering lawful basis selection, data minimization, and retention limits for this exact use case ([Gable: GDPR and Workplace Analytics](https://www.gable.to/blog/post/gdpr-workplace-analytics)).
- **Works councils and jurisdictional variation.** In much of continental Europe (Germany, Netherlands, France), works councils (Betriebsrat, ondernemingsraad) have **co-determination rights** over the introduction of any system capable of monitoring employee performance or behavior — meaning an organization cannot simply deploy transcript-mining or Slack-mining tooling without formal works council consultation and approval, and Dutch and German regulators have published explicit guidance on this ([Hogan Lovells: The Works Council Privacy Booklet](https://www.hoganlovells.com/en/publications/the-works-council-privacy-booklet-new-guidance-published-by-dutch-dpa), [Eurofound: Employee Monitoring — A Moving Target for Regulation](https://www.eurofound.europa.eu/en/publications/all/employee-monitoring-moving-target-regulation)). This is a materially higher bar than in the US, where such consultation requirements are far rarer, and any global rollout of unstructured-signal mining must be jurisdiction-aware rather than uniform.
- **How existing tools handle this ethically.** Microsoft Viva Insights is explicitly designed to surface only **aggregated, de-identified organizational trends** by default (individual-level data is visible only to the individual themselves, not managers, unless explicitly reconfigured), positioning itself around wellbeing rather than surveillance ([Microsoft Viva Insights](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-viva/insights), [Microsoft Viva Workplace Analytics and Employee Feedback](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-viva/workplace-analytics-and-feedback)). Jellyfish and Swarmia similarly market themselves around team-level and org-level rollups rather than individual performance scoring, an industry-wide response to the reputational and legal risk of engineer-level surveillance metrics.

### 7.3 Recommended Architecture for Ingesting Unstructured Sources

1. **Connector layer**: read-only, scoped API connectors to Gmail/Microsoft Graph (email metadata and, where consented, content), Zoom/Teams/Google Meet transcript exports, Slack/Teams message history (via enterprise export APIs, not user-level scraping), and Jira/Linear/Azure Boards ticket APIs — each gated by explicit data-governance approval and, where required by jurisdiction, works-council sign-off before activation.
2. **Transcription/normalization layer**: for audio/video sources, a transcription step (many orgs already have this via Gong/Fireflies/Otter/Granola, so integration rather than rebuilding transcription is preferable) followed by speaker-diarization and redaction of PII (names replaced with role/team labels) before any content leaves the ingestion boundary.
3. **NLP/LLM extraction layer**: a structured-extraction LLM pass (schema-constrained, similar to the `extract_many` pattern used elsewhere in this research) that classifies each transcript/ticket/thread against the Section 4 rubric dimensions — e.g., tagging a meeting segment as evidence for "Measurement: Level 1 (ad hoc feedback)" with a confidence score and a redacted supporting excerpt, never raw verbatim PII-laden text in the final report.
4. **Signal-to-rubric mapping layer**: aggregates classified snippets across many meetings/tickets/threads into a per-dimension, per-tier evidence count (e.g., "14 independent mentions of 'manual catalog update' across Q2 meetings and tickets" as corroborating evidence for a Level 1 Service Catalog score).
5. **Reconciliation with "hard" signals**: the soft-signal layer's per-dimension score is compared against the static/API-scan score from Section 6. Three outcomes are possible:
   - **Confirmation** — soft and hard signals agree (e.g., code shows no IaC and meetings confirm "we do everything manually") → high-confidence score.
   - **Contradiction flagged for review** — e.g., the catalog shows 100% metadata population (hard signal: Level 3) but meeting transcripts repeatedly reference "nobody trusts the catalog data" (soft signal: Level 1) → this exact pattern is the single most valuable output of combining the two layers, since it surfaces "maturity theater" (a populated but unused/untrusted system) that pure code-scanning would completely miss.
   - **Gap-fill** — a dimension the hard-signal layer cannot observe at all (e.g., *why* adoption is mandate-driven versus organic) is populated only by the soft-signal layer, with lower confidence weighting than a corroborated hard signal but still reported.
   Confidence weighting should favor hard signals for objective/structural dimensions (IaC coverage, MCP authentication) and favor soft signals for culture/intent dimensions (adoption motivation, trust in tooling), consistent with DORA's own finding that even its rigorous quantitative survey instrument still relies on self-report Likert items for exactly these intent-and-culture questions ([DORA 2024 Research Questions](https://dora.dev/research/2024/questions/)).

## 8. Template Maturity Survey Instrument

The following survey is designed to be sent to engineering/platform stakeholders to validate automated-scan findings and fill gaps automation cannot reliably detect (intent, culture, political context, roadmap). Each question is tagged **[CONFIRM]** (designed to triangulate/validate what automated scanning already inferred) or **[GAP-FILL]** (designed to surface information automated scanning structurally cannot see). This structure follows real-world precedent: DORA's own annual survey mixes objective behavioral questions (deployment frequency, approval latency) with attitudinal Likert items ([DORA 2024 Research Questions](https://dora.dev/research/2024/questions/)); Microsoft's Platform Engineering Capability Model survey is explicitly designed to be completed either as a live online survey or a manual self-assessment chart, and used to plot current-vs-desired-state per capability rather than a single static score ([Microsoft Learn: Platform Engineering Capability Model](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/platform-engineering/platform-engineering-capability-model)); and the Team Topologies Assessment is a live, standardized online instrument used industry-wide to self-diagnose "fast flow" organizational readiness ([Team Topologies Assessment](https://survey.teamtopologies.com/tt-assessment)). A German productivity-tooling vendor, Echometer, has also published ready-to-use DORA-and-SPACE-aligned retrospective survey templates combining Likert-scale and open-ended prompts, following the same mixed-method pattern recommended here ([Echometer: DORA & SPACE Metrics Templates](https://echometerapp.com/en/dora-space-metrics-templates-questions-survey-test-retrospective/)).

### Section A: Infrastructure & Cloud Posture

1. **[CONFIRM]** Which of the following best describes your organization's primary infrastructure hosting model? *(Single choice: All on-premises / Single public cloud / Multi-cloud, unplanned / Multi-cloud, deliberate strategy / Hybrid on-prem + cloud)*
2. **[CONFIRM]** What percentage of your production infrastructure do you believe is defined in version-controlled Infrastructure as Code (Terraform, CDKTF, Helm, Kubernetes manifests)? *(Multiple choice bands: 0-20% / 21-50% / 51-80% / 81-99% / 100%)*
3. **[GAP-FILL]** Does your organization have a documented strategy or roadmap for GPU/AI-compute capacity (on-prem clusters, reserved cloud GPU instances, or both)? *(Yes, documented and funded / Informal plans only / No plan / Not sure)*
4. **[GAP-FILL]** In the last 6 months, has your team experienced a production incident caused by infrastructure that existed outside of your IaC/version control ("shadow infrastructure")? *(Yes / No / Not sure)* — open text: describe briefly.
5. **[GAP-FILL, open-ended]** What is the single biggest infrastructure or cloud-strategy decision your organization is currently debating that has *not* yet been reflected in any documentation or code?

### Section B: Developer Productivity Metrics Capture

1. **[CONFIRM]** Which DORA metrics does your organization actively track on a recurring (weekly/monthly) basis? *(Select all: Deployment frequency / Lead time for changes / Change failure rate / Mean time to restore / None of these)*
2. **[CONFIRM]** How is developer productivity data primarily gathered today? *(Single choice: Not gathered at all / Manual, ad hoc surveys / Semi-automated via spreadsheets or scripts / Automated via a dedicated platform such as LinearB, Jellyfish, Swarmia, or Faros AI)*
3. **[GAP-FILL]** How much do engineers on your team *trust* the productivity metrics currently reported to leadership? *(5-point Likert: Not at all — Completely)*
4. **[GAP-FILL]** Do engineers on your team feel productivity metrics are ever used punitively (e.g., individual performance ranking) rather than for systemic improvement? *(Yes / No / Unsure)*
5. **[GAP-FILL, open-ended]** Describe a recent example where a productivity metric (or the lack of one) directly influenced a decision on your team.

### Section C: Service Catalog / Self-Service Capabilities

1. **[CONFIRM]** Does your organization maintain a central service catalog (e.g., Backstage, Cortex, OpsLevel, Port.io, or an internal equivalent)? *(Yes, and it's kept current / Yes, but it's frequently stale / No)*
2. **[CONFIRM]** What percentage of new services created in the last quarter were created via a standardized "golden path" template rather than built from scratch? *(0% / 1-25% / 26-50% / 51-75% / 76-100%)*
3. **[GAP-FILL]** How would you describe the demand for new golden-path templates or self-service capabilities relative to what the platform team can currently deliver? *(5-point Likert: Far exceeds supply — Fully met)*
4. **[GAP-FILL]** What is the most common reason a team bypasses the service catalog or self-service tooling and does something manually instead?
5. **[GAP-FILL, open-ended]** If you could add one new self-service capability to the platform tomorrow, what would it be and why?

### Section D: AI Agent Governance, Security, and RBAC

1. **[CONFIRM]** Does your organization maintain a documented, complete inventory of all MCP servers or equivalent AI-agent tool integrations in use? *(Yes, centrally maintained / Partial/informal / No)*
2. **[CONFIRM]** What authentication standard do your AI agent/MCP integrations use? *(OAuth 2.1 with PKCE / API keys or tokens only / No authentication / Not sure)*
3. **[CONFIRM]** Is there a documented, organization-wide policy on which AI coding tools (e.g., Claude Code, Cursor, GitHub Copilot) are approved for use, and under what conditions? *(Yes, clear and communicated / Exists but poorly communicated / No policy exists)*
4. **[GAP-FILL]** Have you or your team ever been blocked from adopting an AI tool or agent integration due to unclear approval processes? *(Yes / No)* — if yes, describe the bottleneck.
5. **[GAP-FILL, open-ended]** What AI-agent-related security or governance concern worries you most that current tooling/policy does not yet address?

### Section E: Agent Skill / Tool Discovery and Reuse

1. **[CONFIRM]** Does your repository (or the majority of repositories you work in) contain an `AGENT.md`/`AGENTS.md` file or equivalent agent-configuration file (`.cursorrules`, `.claude/`, Copilot instructions)? *(Yes, in most repos / Yes, in some repos / No / Not sure)*
2. **[CONFIRM]** How are useful AI agent prompts, skills, or MCP server configurations shared across teams today? *(Centralized registry/marketplace / Informal sharing via Slack/docs / Not shared, everyone builds their own / Not applicable)*
3. **[GAP-FILL]** On a scale of 1-5, how much duplicated effort do you believe exists across teams in building similar AI agent tooling or prompts? *(1 = none, 5 = severe duplication)*
4. **[GAP-FILL, open-ended]** Describe an AI agent skill, prompt, or MCP integration you built that you believe other teams could reuse but currently don't know exists.

### Section F: Culture, Process, and Team Topologies Alignment

1. **[CONFIRM]** Which best describes your platform team's structure? *(No dedicated platform team / Informal platform function / Dedicated platform team without product-management practices / Dedicated platform team operating as a product with roadmap and feedback loops)*
2. **[CONFIRM]** Why did your team originally start using the internal platform? *(Top-down mandate / Clear intrinsic value / Peer/community recommendation / No clear reason — adoption was erratic)*
3. **[GAP-FILL]** How would you rate your team's current cognitive load — the number of distinct systems, tools, and responsibilities you must track day-to-day? *(5-point Likert, aligned to the [Team Cognitive Load Assessment](https://github.com/TeamTopologies/Team-Cognitive-Load-Assessment) methodology)*
4. **[GAP-FILL]** Is there a recurring forum (platform review board, architecture guild, AI governance committee) where infrastructure and AI-tooling decisions are discussed and documented? *(Yes, regular and well-attended / Exists but inconsistent / No such forum exists)*
5. **[GAP-FILL, open-ended]** What is the biggest *organizational* (not technical) obstacle to improving platform or AI-agent maturity at your company right now?

### Notes on Survey Design and Deployment

- **Scoring**: multiple-choice/Likert responses in Sections A-F map directly onto the Level 0-4 tiers in Section 4's rubric, allowing automatic quantitative scoring; open-ended responses should be routed through the same LLM extraction layer described in Section 7.3 to classify qualitative context against rubric dimensions and flag contradictions with the automated-scan findings.
- **Targeting**: distribute separately to (a) platform/infrastructure engineers, (b) product/feature engineering teams (the platform's "customers"), and (c) engineering leadership, since the CNCF and Puppet research consistently finds systematic perception gaps between platform teams and their consumers on adoption motivation and measurement trust — comparing response patterns across these three groups is itself a maturity signal.
- **Cadence**: align with the continuous-scan/quarterly-survey cadence recommended in Section 6.2, and consider adopting DORA's own free **Quick Check** instrument as a lightweight, already-validated starting point for the DevOps-specific portions of Section B ([DORA Quick Check](https://dora.dev/quickcheck/)).
